About Me

Hey, I'm Hannah Green. I study Computer Science and Mathematics at Middlebury College in the scenic Green Mountains of Vermont. I love software development because it allows me to bring my interests, passions, and creativity to life. The entire development process - taking an idea and coding it into existence - is immensely rewarding to me.

I grew up in Oakland, CA, and had ample time outside of school to pursue extracurricular interests that sparked lifelong hobbies. My passions for surfing, skiing, mountain biking, singing, gaming, and basketball were all born in the East Bay.

As a Computer Science major and Math minor at Middlebury, I take courses pertaining predominantly to my areas of study. However, my liberal arts education has allowed me to take classes in a wide variety of topics, from the Psychology of Happiness to the Aztec Empire to Sherlock Holmes in the Media. I think diversity should be an intrinsic part of any education, and am thankful to have broadened my academic perspective throughout my time at college.

Experience
Comapany Summer 2017
Position City, ST

This past summer, I interned for Booz Allen Hamilton's Cyber branch based in Annapolis Junction, MD. I was one of six engineering interns, and over the course of the 10 week intership we developed a Chrome extension to detect fake news using machine learning. We were also featured on the news!

Our tool is a MEAN stack application that communicates with a powerful Java server to handle heavy data processing. I contributed to all parts of our system as the summer progressed; I designed the UI using JavaScript and HTML/CSS, wrote a web scraper in NodeJS, and developed several mertics in Java that served as the backbone of our analytic engine. Additionally, I led many team discussions that paved the way for any subsequent technical work.

The summer concluded with a formal presentation to Booz Allen senior leadership. This presentation was part of an internal competition know as the Summer Games, and our team was awarded 1st place overall out of the 17 teams and 80+ interns competing! All in all, this experience taught me both the foundations of software development and essential team collaboration skills. Our team's ability to work together allowed the whole to surpass the sum of its parts, and I believe is what propelled us to our 1st place finish.

Company 2 Time - time
PositionPlace, ST

In my free time at school I created Fours, a free KenKen puzzle generator available on the App Store. I loved playing KenKen puzzles (similar to math Sudokus) in the New York Times, but wanted to solve more than just one a day. So, I embarked on my journey that led to Fours, which now allows me to play as many puzzles as I want - the app can generate over 600,000 possible board combinations.

I began the process knowing nothing about iOS development, and the path from concept to published product taught me invaluable lessons about both software design and myself. I began the project by inventing my own method of generating unique puzzle boards, then implemented my algorithm in Swift as the core component of the app. I then built additonal features to help the app become more than just a puzzle generator - features such as challenge modes, global leaderboards, and a step-by-step tutorial that helps teach newcomers how to play.

To market my new app, I taught myself web development from scratch and built Fours a landing page. The website shows off the app's various capabilities, and integrates with Firebase to display the highest scoring players worldwide. Even though I created Fours simply as a way to play more KenKen puzzles, I hope it can now become a platform to share my passion for KenKen with others.

For more information, check out www.getfoursapp.com

Company 3 Time - time
PositionPlace, ST

In the summer of 2016 I worked for ACG Research, a leading analyst firm in the telecommunications industry. Over the course of the summer, I was an integral part of a 4 person team responsible for kickstarting ACG Buisness Analytics, a new branch focused on developing software to revolutionize industry-standard data analysis methods.

Within our small business analytics team, I played a significant role in the early software development process. First, I conducted market research in various broadband usage sectors and built a custom database that acted as the foundation of our software's analytic algorithms.

With our data collected and stored, I then created multiple Excel models to mimic the functionality of our software under development. This type of Excel model is widely used for standard data analysis, so our software's goal of replacing these models was founded on it accurately reproducing their results.

As part of a small, agile team, I participated in meetings twice a week and I provided input in the business, software, and analytical aspects of our project. I reported directly to Ray Mota, who provided me with countless bits of wisdom and advice along the way. By the end of the summer, I had learned an incredible amount about developing software in a small team and building advanced models in Excel.

Company 4 Time-time
PositionPlace, ST

In the summer of 2015 I interned at Medivation Inc. (recently acquired by Pfizer), a biopharmaceutical company located the heart of San Francisco's Financial district. At Medivation I worked as a member of their Statistical Programming team, and was responsible for creating standardized data libraries from their internal databases.

Tasked with the somewhat tedious process of building standard libaries of pharmaceutical data, I decided to automate the process. I leveraged my past experience with Excel to create a macro that essentially did the job for me, pulling data from various sheets in multiple files and compiling it into a centralized database. At the conclusion of the internship, I presented my project to the team's senior leadership and showed them how they could use my tool to create databases for future work.

I wrote the macro with VBA, which helped to reinforce the single semester of programming experience I had at the time. Looking back, this database scraper was very similar to various web scrapers I would eventually write, and ended up serving me well in my future programming ventures.
